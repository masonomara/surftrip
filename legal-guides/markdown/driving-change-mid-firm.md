## Why change is so hard— and how to make it easier


Resistance to change is a natural human response—one that is
particularly pronounced within the legal profession. Understanding
the roots of resistance is essential for successfully navigating change
management and fostering a culture of transformation.


**The hidden power of legal mental models**

Lawyers are trained to rely on precedent, standards and mental
models to solve problems, reduce risk and maintain high standards.
These models, grounded in tradition, also create inertia and resistance
to new ways of operating. Lawyers tend to favor the familiar, often
sticking to established practices—even when those practices are
inefficient. This preference for the “devil they know” is driven by the
fear of the unknown and the discomfort that comes with challenging
established norms. This resistance is not simply about reluctance
to change, but a fear of losing the stability and certainty that
tradition provides.


**Escape “shiny object syndrome” with strategic intent**

Today’s legal leaders are increasingly called upon to adopt new
technologies to solve problems and enhance the effectiveness of their
practices. Few lawyers have a strategic framework for evaluating
new initiatives, which means that many fall into the trap of “shiny
object syndrome.” This occurs when firms implement changes that
don’t align with their long-term goals, resulting in change fatigue and
wasted resources. To avoid this, it’s essential to have a clear process for
evaluating any new initiative. Before diving into a change, ask: “How
does this align with our strategy? How will it deliver measurable ROI?”
Change should ultimately lead to a transformation—one that benefits
the firm and its clients in a tangible way.

**Overcome perfectionism, people-pleasing, and the fear**
**of mistakes**


The legal profession often attracts individuals with a strong drive
for perfectionism and a desire to meet the expectations of both
clients and colleagues. FUD (Fear, Uncertainty, and Doubt) represents
a set of anxieties that contribute to resistance to change, particularly
when outcomes are unknown or unclear. FUD is often a hidden cause
of hesitation and delay in decision-making.


Fear of making mistakes—whether in delivering services or adopting
new ways of working—is a common blocker to change. Leaders can
overcome this by reframing mistakes as opportunities for growth,
not failures. Creating a safe environment for experimentation, where
individuals can take calculated risks without fear of judgment, is key
to encouraging change.

**The Change Equation: A formula for transformation:**


The Change Equation [1] provides a critical framework for overcoming resistance:


**Dissatisfaction (D) x Vision (V) x First Steps (F) > Resistance (R)**


This formula illustrates that all three factors must work in harmony to effectively
counter resistance. It’s a multiplication equation, so if any factor is zero, or negative,
then the product will be zero or negative and resistance will win—and meaningful
change will not happen.


Dig into dissatisfaction (D)

To initiate change, it’s essential to identify and articulate the pain points
and inefficiencies that exist within the current state. Leaders should
actively engage with their teams to uncover frustrations, asking practical
questions such as:


                  - “What’s the most frustrating part of your day right now? Why?”


                  - “If you could fix one thing, what would it be? Why?”


Common pain points in law firms include time-consuming manual
processes, redundant data entry, and client dissatisfaction with outdated
communication methods. By clearly identifying these issues, you can
create a sense of urgency for change.


Craft a compelling vision (V)

A strong vision provides a clear and motivating picture of what the
future could look like after the change is implemented. It answers
the “why”—articulating the benefits of the change for the team, the
clients, and the firm as a whole. The most effective visions are co-created
and fostered through regular communication, one-on-one meetings,
and small group discussions. If your team cannot readily explain your
vision, and find motivation in it, then it isn’t effective. Engaging people
in the vision-building process encourages buy-in and fosters a sense
of ownership. For example, if dissatisfaction stems from inefficient
billing processes, the vision could focus on increased productivity,
faster payments, and improved client satisfaction—and most
importantly, the transformation the team members will experience
when the change is fully implemented.

Taking meaningful first steps (F)

Initiating change doesn’t require having all the steps identified.
You simply need to identify tangible, achievable first steps. The most
complicated problems are often solved with a series of painfully simple
steps. Achieving “easy wins” helps build momentum and reduces
the feeling of being overwhelmed. Early steps could include having
a discovery conversation about potential solutions or testing a new
tool with a select team. Each step should be designed to lead to the next,
creating a steady path toward transformation.

By aligning goals with the firm’s vision, prioritizing what truly
matters, and involving your team, you’ll create OKRs and KPIs
that resonate and drive action. For mid-sized law firms, this
structured approach ensures that every objective moves the
firm closer to its strategic ambitions, fosters accountability,
and inspires your team to work toward shared success.


Resistance to change (R)

Resistance often stems from inertia—the fear of moving from a familiar
state to an unfamiliar one. By understanding the psychological and
strategic factors that drive resistance to change, leaders can proactively
address them and set the stage for a successful transformation. Through
cultivating dissatisfaction with the current state, crafting a compelling
vision for the future, and taking manageable first steps, law firms can
overcome resistance and embrace change that benefits both the firm
and its clients.

When applying the Change Equation, if any of the factors
(Dissatisfaction, Vision, or First Steps) is weak or missing, the result will
be zero, and resistance will not be overcome. That’s why it’s crucial to
have a strong commitment to all three components to drive change.

Before we dive into Part 2, here’s where the Change Equation really
starts to click. When something’s not working—when change is stalling
or people are resisting—it’s tempting to jump to tactics. But real traction
comes when you go deeper. That’s why I often ask my clients to go
five levels down—keep asking “why?” until you uncover what’s truly
driving (or blocking) change. Because when you get to the root of the _D_
(dissatisfaction), the _V_ (vision), the _F_ (first steps), or the _R_ (resistance),
you unlock real power. That’s where lasting change starts—not with
surface-level fixes, but with a clear understanding of what’s really
going on.

## Embracing change—what to expect after you start


The Change Equation—Dissatisfaction x Vision x First Steps >
Resistance—highlights how dissatisfaction with current practices,
a compelling vision for the future, and tangible first steps can
collectively overcome resistance. Once your firm takes those first steps,
it’s worth taking a moment to recognize what progress towards real,
impactful change looks like.

The change management process often comes with its own challenges
to manage and overcome. The concept of the Change Curve illustrates
some of these challenges, particularly the emotional responses that
your team may experience during a period of change.

The Change Curve is divided into three main stages:

**• Stage 1:** This initial stage is characterized by **shock** and **denial.**

Even if your team has been involved in creating a vision for change
and those first steps, when a change is first introduced, individuals
may experience a sense of shock or disbelief. They might deny
the need for change or its potential impact. During this stage,
**performance typically sees an initial dip** as people process
the news and resist acknowledging the new reality.

**• Stage 2:** As the initial shock and denial subside, individuals

move into Stage 2, which involves **Anger** and **Depression** .
Resistance becomes more active, and people may feel frustrated,
resentful, or angry about the change. This can lead to a significant
decline in morale, and **performance may drop even further** .
Feelings of loss and uncertainty can contribute to a sense of
depression or disengagement.

**• Stage 3:** The final stage of the Change Curve involves **Acceptance**
and **Integration** . As individuals begin to understand and accept
the change, they start to adjust and find ways to work within the
new environment. **Performance gradually begins to recover and**
**eventually integrates at a new level** . People start to embrace the
changes and may even see the potential benefits.

These stages aren’t optional—they’re part of the human experience
of change, much like the cycles of grief. Everyone goes through them.
Some move quickly. Others get stuck in Stage 1 or 2, or even slide
backwards when the pressure builds. That’s why effective leaders don’t
try to force people forward. They stay curious. They ask why someone
might be resisting, and look for the unmet needs underneath. Often,
simply recognizing a person’s natural tendency to ask, “ _What’s in it for_
_me?_ ” can unlock a path forward. When you see these stages for what
they are—predictable, human responses—you can meet people where
they are, guide them through the tough parts, and help them move
toward acceptance, performance, and lasting change.

## Real-world insights from Koenig|Dunne


Koenig|Dunne, a 20-employee Omaha-based Divorce firm, has firsthand
experience applying change management principles with their team.
Their insights offer valuable, real-world guidance for mid-sized law firms
looking to implement effective change strategies.


**Strategic rollout with test groups**

Angela Lennon, Partner and COO, and Michaela Seidl, Advanced
Certified Paralegal and Paralegal Manager, at Koenig|Dunne both
emphasize the effectiveness of beginning change initiatives with
a test group of team members who are most receptive to new changes.
This approach allows firms to identify potential issues early on and
develop best practices before a broader rollout. Michaela also highlights
the importance of including individuals who may be more resistant
to change. By bringing in those who are hesitant, firms can gain
valuable insights into potential barriers and better address concerns
from all angles.


**Addressing resistance through open dialogue and curiosity**

At Koenig|Dunne resistance is met with active listening and a genuine
effort to understand the underlying concerns. In one case, Angela was
herself initially resistant to adopting electronic draft reviews at the
firm. As the one resistant to change herself, she participated in
the team dialogue to articulate potential challenges, and to ultimately
help shape a compelling vision for how to move forward and overcome
perceived hurdles. Through patient, open dialogue and curiosity, the
team worked together to uncover what was truly getting in the way.
It is through this process that teams can better address concerns and
foster a more receptive environment for change.

**Navigating common sticking points with empathy**
**and transparency**

Fear of incompetence, especially when learning new technologies
or processes, is a major barrier to change. Michaela notes that “people
don’t want to feel stupid.” Additionally, worries about job security
due to automation or the ingrained mindset of “this is the way we’ve
always done it” can significantly hinder progress. To address these
concerns, it’s essential to create a safe space for open dialogue.
Transparent communication about the rationale behind the changes
and addressing potential fears head-on is key to overcoming these
barriers and building trust.


**Understanding the personal connection: “What’s in it for me?”**
**(WIIFM)**

Change initiatives must clearly articulate the personal benefits for each
team member. Everyone is motivated by their own needs and priorities,
which is why it’s crucial to ask: What’s in it for me? This question serves
as the filter through which team members evaluate change. By framing
the change in terms of individual benefits—such as less time spent on
administrative tasks or more efficient workflows—leaders can increase
buy-in and support.


**Setting SMART goals for focused implementation**

Koenig|Dunne employs a goal-oriented approach to change. When
introducing new systems or processes, defining **SMART** goals— **S** pecific,
**M** easurable, **A** chievable, **R** ealistic, and **T** imely—provides clarity
and ensures progress can be tracked. For example, implementing
a new client intake system might involve setting specific metrics for
success, such as reducing intake time by 30% over a three-month period.
This structured approach ensures that the impact of change
can be clearly evaluated and adjusted if necessary.

## Sustaining continuous improvement


Change is not a one-time event; it’s a continuous journey that
requires a commitment to ongoing improvement and adaptation.
To successfully sustain change, law firms must foster a culture
of learning, responsiveness, and client-centered focus.


**The impact of leadership style on change adoption**

Leadership style plays a critical role in the success of change initiatives.
Different approaches can either facilitate or hinder the adoption of
change. A **coaching** or **affiliative** leadership style—one that is supportive,
empathetic, and focuses on building relationships—tends to make
change easier and more sustainable. In contrast, **pacesetting** or **coercive**
leadership styles, which emphasize high expectations and control,
may lead to short-term compliance but are less effective over time
and can create resistance. Leaders should be mindful of their style
and adapt it to encourage buy-in and foster a smoother transition. [2]

**Implementing after-action reviews for learning and growth**

The **After-Action Review (AAR)** is a valuable tool for reflecting on
the successes and challenges of change initiatives. By asking four
key questions, firms can assess the impact of the change and learn
from both successes and setbacks:

_• What did we expect to happen?_

_• What actually happened?_

_• What was different, and why was it different?_

_• What do we do differently next time?_

The focus should be on objective analysis and identifying areas for
future growth, not on assigning blame. AARs create opportunities
for continuous learning, allowing firms to adapt and improve their
processes over time. [3]

**Maintaining client trust and satisfaction during times**
**of change**

Many changes within law firms are implemented with the goal
of improving client service. However, periods of transition—such as
attorney turnover or the introduction of new systems—can create
uncertainty for clients. It’s essential to have a clear strategy in place
and proactively communicate with clients to alleviate concerns.

the same high standards of service during times of change.

they need uninterrupted time to give it the proper attention.
This approach ensures clients feel valued while also maintaining
a level of service that is consistent with their needs.

**Glossary of key terms and concepts**


**Resistance to change:** The natural response to change, often driven by
mental models such as fear of the unknown, perfectionism, and risk
aversion. Common contributing factors include precedent preference,
fear of loss, FUD (Fear, Uncertainty, and Doubt), people-pleasing,
shiny object syndrome, and change fatigue.


**The Change Equation:** A formula illustrating the conditions necessary
for successful change. It consists of **Dissatisfaction (D)**, **Vision (V)**,
and **First Steps (F)**, with their multiplication overcoming **Resistance**
**(R)** : D × V × F > R.


**SMART goals:** A framework for setting clear, actionable goals
during change initiatives: **Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic,**
and **Timely** . SMART goals help track progress and ensure focus on the
desired outcomes.


**WIIFM (What’s in it for me?):** The personal benefit or perceived
advantage an individual believes they will gain from adopting
a particular change. WIIFM is critical in gaining buy-in from
team members.


**Tips and traps in change management:** Recognizing the
counterproductivity of excessive time pressure on creativity,
understanding **WIIFM** for team members, clarifying the **Why**
behind change (Simon Sinek’s approach), and adapting leadership
culture and style to facilitate smoother transitions.


**After-action reviews:** A structured process for evaluating change
initiatives. It involves asking what was expected, what actually
happened, what was different, and how to improve in the future.
The focus is on objective learning rather than assigning blame.


**Psychological safety:** Creating an environment where team
members feel comfortable taking risks, voicing concerns,
and learning from mistakes—essential for effective change,
especially during challenging transitions.


**Change fatigue:** The exhaustion and resistance that result from
undergoing too many organizational changes in a short period,
leading to diminished engagement and productivity.
